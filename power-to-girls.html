The Story of Power to Girls' Aisha Addo

Last week, I had the pleasure to talk with Aisha Addo, founder at Power to Girls Foundation. Power to Girls is a social enterprise that empowers marginalised girls in Canada and Ghana through a variety of programs and mentorship. Discover Aisha's journey as a social entrepreneur, and how she has helped these young girls achieve their full potential, through this short interview!Â 

Can you tell me about yourself?

Thank you so much for having me. My name is Aisha, and I am the founder of an organisation called Power to Girls. We provide leadership, personal development, mental health, and entrepreneurship training to support Black and marginalised girls. We have been doing this for the past eight years, and it is something that I care deeply about.

Can you tell me more about the activities that you do to support these young girls?

Yes, absolutely. We have a partnership with the Toronto Catholic District School Board to deliver our programs within the schools.

We focus mainly on girls that are in middle school (6th to 8th grades), and essentially run a series of programs and workshops that highlight and address our four main pillars: mental health, leadership, mentorship and entrepreneurship.

We ensure that the girls are given a space that allows for them to be themselves, pick up on all these programs, and become whoever they want to become in the future.

What were your drivers and motivation to start the foundation?

I came to Canada as an immigrant, and my first experience was in high school. I was going through my teenage years, and could not find a space that allowed me to tap into the questions I had, as a source of empowerment. Seeing that Black girls did not have places to express themselves was a first key motivator for me to start Power to Girls.

Everything shifted when I ended up in foster care (from 15 to 18 years old). I remember my foster mom having a conversation with me and explaining to me that the system that has been assigned to me did not necessarily expect much of me. Basically, I was expected to either become a teenage mom, a dropout, a drug addict, etc.Â 

I remember saying to myself "I'm not going to be a stereotype!".

These two experiences coupled with other ones became the driving forces for Power to Girls, as I realised that even girls that might not necessarily find themselves in foster care still need sources of inspiration and have those moments of questioning about who they are.

Can a young girl get support after the middle-school programs?

We focus on young girls in middle school, but we still provide support later on, when they get into high school for instance.

In the next couple of months, we are launching My Power App, an application that focuses specifically on connecting girls to mentors, and providing support through the app itself.

So, we are really proud of the beautiful cycle it becomes. Indeed, the girls that participate in our programs always end up coming back as mentors or facilitators. The majority of the facilitators that we have now are girls that have participated in our program in the past. Once you have joined Power to Girls, you are part of the Power to Girls family.

What effects have you noticed in the lives of the girls that you are helping as a result of your initiatives?

This is always a very interesting question because our programs focus on self-reflection and personal development.

It is always beautiful to notice the changes within our girls. We have programs where some people are super quiet in the beginning but, getting closer to the end of the programs, they become more comfortable in who they are and more expressive. Also, we have had girls that have started their own businesses in one of our entrepreneurship programs, and we have seen them getting the confidence to share and talk about their businesses.

So, we witness changes in their communication style and self-confidence. We are also able to see them being comfortable and beautiful in their own body, and feeling confident enough to speak up when something does not feel right for them.

I find your organisation interesting, because you provide these girls with a holistic support system, including a set of complementary tools that let them blossom. I think it helps them today, and it will also open opportunities for them in the future. Also, it is great to see that they are able to support other girls later on as well.

Let's talk more about your journey as a social entrepreneur. What steps did you take to create the foundation, and how long did it take to kick start it?

Yes, that is a really good question. I started Power to Girls when I was in college. So, it started based on my passion of not having girls experience some of the things that I had in Canada and society in general.

Then, I realised that I wanted to turn it to more than a passion down the road. So, I wondered, how do I turn this passion into purpose? How can I turn this project into something that is beyond myself, that will still strive and go on even when I am not around? The journey has not been easy: you have to find the right people, support, and many other things related to the entrepreneurial journey itself.

Currently, we have 3 full-time staff, 15 facilitators that we can always count on, and mentors. We also have folks that want to be a part of our organisation, we provide them with training and then, they go back and provide training themselves. So, we are now running an amazing, oiled machine which not only provides employment, but also support.

I am recognising the magnitude of what we are doing, now that I am speaking to you. It has been a process, and we did not get here overnight. It was something that we worked hard to make happen.

I am also able to share this because I have got some great people that believe in the vision and the work that we are doing. I think it is crucial to work with folks that are smarter than you, but also actually care about what you are working on as it generates a sense of ownership.

It is rare to see something happen overnight. It is a good thing to work on something that you care about to be able to enjoy the effort that you will have to put on. Even for the people that appear to have succeeded overnight, it usually involves a lot of experiences, maybe some failures, a lot of work, etc.

So, what were your biggest challenges at the different stages of your journey?

One of the biggest challenges I have had is accessing capital. When the need arises, I do not mind taking out of my pocket, because it is something that I care deeply about.Â 

Otherwise, since I originally studied accounting in school, and social work was not my first choice, accessing the resources, skills and support that would allow us to scale the organisation is challenging as well.

How are you tackling these challenges?

It is a matter of networking and trying to find different opportunities for us to build partnerships. Also, applying to more grants.

Otherwise, having the platform that we are currently creating is also allowing us to automate things.

Finally, we look for people that are going to become champions of the organisation and support us, throughout our growth and journey. We are so grateful for the partners that we have now and the ones to come.

What is the status of your organisation? Do you generate revenues to sustain your purpose?

We are a not-for-profit organisation registered in Canada, and in Ghana as well. We do generate revenues from the sale of our merchandise, which are funneled back into the organisation.

How has the pandemic affected your work and how have you adapted so far?

We are closely tied to the school and education system. Whatever happens to the school system indirectly happens to us. Also, as a result of the pandemic, some funding has been put on hold.

When the pandemic first started, we were very focused on ensuring that people and our communities were safe. Now, we are adapting into a more online module, which has been great for us as we have been able to reach more people.

Do you have any strategies to tackle the challenges resulting from moving to online programs?

Reinforcing who we are as individuals to create a sense of connection. Also, we continuously remind people that it is safe space to talk, and that everything we are doing is for their benefit.Â 

What do you feel have been your greatest achievements as a social entrepreneur?

Right now, I am really excited about the app, and just what we have done with it in the middle of the pandemic, because it is something that I have always wanted to do.

Also, I am very proud of my team, the people that I got the opportunity to work with, and my girls for always showing up and being present.
Â 
Finally, I am proud of myself for sticking it through, because it has not been an easy journey and many times we had wanted to give up, but we kept going.

What is your vision for the years to come and how do you plan to materialise it?

At a personal level, being at peace and ensuring that everything I do stems from passion and love.

From an organisational standpoint, launching the app, impacting and supporting more girls, and expanding our work into places we have not been yet.
Â 
The idea is taking it one day at a time, being conscious that we are capable ofÂ  accomplishing everything that we set our minds to, and ensuring that we are safe while we do so.
